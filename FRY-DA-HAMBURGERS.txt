In mythology, coins placed on the eyes (or in the mouth) of the dead, known as Charon's obol, were a payment for the ferryman to cross the River Styx into the underworld; this practice was common in ancient Greece and Rome, though later cultures adopted it for practical reasons like keeping eyelids shut to prevent wandering souls, blending myth with physical necessity, notes.
Mythological Significance (Ancient Greece & Rome)
Payment for Passage: The coins, often obols, were a bribe or fee for Charon, the ferryman, to transport the deceased's soul across the Styx to Hades.
Preventing Wandering Souls: Without payment, the soul was believed to wander the earth for a hundred years, unable to reach the afterlife.
Restoring Sight: Some believed the coins restored sight in the afterlife, symbolizing wealth and vision in the underworld, as per.
Practical & Cultural Reasons (Later Eras)
Keeping Eyes Closed: In many cultures, including Victorian England, coins (or stones/shells) were placed on the eyes to physically hold the eyelids shut, preventing the eyes from opening as muscles relaxed after death, which was considered frightening.
Symbolic Wealth: They served as grave goods, ensuring the deceased had something valuable for the journey, notes.
Mythological Blending: The link to Charon's obol was often a later association, with the practical need to keep eyes shut being the primary driver for many cultures, creating a merged tradition, explains.
Key Takeaway: The practice evolved, combining the ancient mythological idea of paying the ferryman with a practical funerary custom to manage the appearance of the deceased, ensuring a peaceful, rather than aimless, journey to the next world, say.
