Biography
Biography
A native Californian, Miranda Cosgrove was raised by Christina (Casey) and Thomas "Tom" Cosgrove, who owns a dry-cleaning small business. She was discovered at a young age singing and dancing around a table in a restaurant, where an agent saw her talent and quickly signed her. Miranda's career started with televised commercials for McDonalds, Burger King, Mello Yello, and more as she auditioned for many roles and finally won her first role as the band's manager in the movie, School of Rock (2003).
While filming School of Rock (2003) in New York, she was informed that her pilot Drake & Josh (2004) had been picked up. She co-starred as Drake and Josh's mischievous, spoiled, bratty troublemaker prankster sister, while making brief appearances on other Nickelodeon shows, before landing in her own show for the network, iCarly (2007). Her iCarly (2007) popularity helped her launch her singing career, and she was named an MTV Pop Rookie for 2009. Her debut album, "Sparks Fly", was released in 2010 and reached #8 on the Billboard 200 chart, along with two EPs: "About You Now" in 2009, and "High Maintenance" in 2011.
In 2010, Miranda expanded upon her voice-over work for the children's movies, including Here Comes Peter Cottontail: The Movie (2005), and the Despicable Me (2010) films and related shorts, as well as expanding her indie film work with Yours, Mine & Ours (2005), Keeping Up with the Steins (2006) and The Wild Stallion (2009). Her TV reach has extended beyond Nickelodeon, with an appearance on the CBS legal drama The Good Wife (2009) during the show's second season. She has served as a brand ambassador for Neutrogena skin care products since 2010.
She lives in Anaheim, California with her family.

In the Bible, the marriage proposal to the bride is generally referred to as betrothal (or erusin in Hebrew custom), a formal and legally binding agreement between the groom (or his representative) and the bride's father, rather than a single, private "proposal" event like in modern Western culture. The woman's consent was also an important aspect of the process.
Biblical Marriage Process
The process involved several stages that differed significantly from modern engagements:
Initiation and Negotiation: A prospective groom (or his father/agent) would approach the bride's father to negotiate terms and agree upon a "bride price" or mohar (a payment or service rendered to the bride's family).
Betrothal (Erusin): Once terms were agreed upon and the price was paid, a covenant was drawn up and signed. This was a legally binding status considered the first stage of marriage. The couple was considered husband and wife, and the betrothal could only be broken by a formal divorce.
The Waiting Period: The groom would then leave to prepare a home or an addition to his father's house for his new wife, a process that often took a year or more. During this time, the bride would prepare her wedding garments and personal items, not knowing the exact day or hour the groom would return for her.
The Wedding: The groom would return at an unexpected time (determined by his father), often with a shout and a trumpet call, leading a procession to the bride's home and then to the wedding feast. The marriage was then formally completed with a ceremony and consummation.
Jesus' "Marriage Proposal" to the Church
In Christian theology, Jesus' relationship with the Church is often described using the metaphor of a bridegroom and bride, drawing parallels to these ancient Jewish customs.
The "Proposal" at the Last Supper: Some theological interpretations view Jesus' actions at the Last Supper, when he offered the "cup of the new covenant in my blood" to his disciples, as a symbolic marriage proposal to humanity (the Church, the bride of Christ). By accepting the cup (through faith and obedience), believers accept his offer of a binding, eternal covenant.
The Bride Price: Jesus paid the "bride price" with his own life and blood on the cross to redeem and sanctify his bride, the Church.
Preparation and Return: Jesus then returned to his Father's house (heaven) to prepare a place for his bride (believers), promising to return one day at an unexpected hour for the "wedding supper of the Lamb" (Revelation 19:7).
The Pledge: He left the Holy Spirit as a "pledge" or guarantee of his return and the future inheritance, which helps believers prepare themselves for his coming.

The Mythological Origin
The Problem: The hero Theseus had to enter the Minotaur's Labyrinth, a complex maze, to slay the beast.
The Solution: Ariadne, daughter of King Minos, gave Theseus a "clew" (a ball of thread).
The Method: Theseus tied one end of the thread to the entrance and unraveled it as he went, allowing him to follow the thread back to safety after defeating the Minotaur.
The Linguistic Evolution
"Clew": The original word meant a ball of thread or yarn.
Metaphorical Shift: The idea of the thread guiding Theseus led to "clew" meaning "that which points the way".
Spelling Change: In the 17th century, the spelling evolved to "clue," which became the standard for the figurative sense (a hint or evidence), while "clew" remained for the literal ball of thread or nautical use (corner of a sail).
